      What you just played

      Difficulty is relational — and a third kind of hard

      A dense mini is over-determined: every letter is checked twice, so most answers are pinned by their crossings whether or not they have a clue. This grid's information floor is 4 — 4 well-chosen clues are enough, and the crossings force the other 6, in a 6-wave cascade the model computes before a clue is written. A word's difficulty is not intrinsic: it is where it sits in the crossing graph, and what its neighbours donate.

      Strip the clue and a third difficulty axis appears — distinct from the two this project already mapped. Not word obscurity (do you know the word — a fairness cliff, not a slope) and not clue obliqueness (how vague the definition — the dominant fair axis when clues exist), but retrievability from a pattern: with the clue gone, the answer's own letters are the clue, and the question is whether you can produce the word from them. A pattern can be lexically forced (one word fits) yet humanly hard — ··CYS uniquely forces MACYS, but you can't enumerate that. So this grid is tuned to keep the letter-clue humanly fair: every answer is a common word, forced with at least 3 of its 5 letters showing — that minvis is the knob.

      The “◇ N still fit” counter is the browser running the same n_candidates check the solver model uses; when it drops to ◆ one word fits, the pattern is uniquely determined and it is yours to deduce — the page never names it. You are told when a slot is solvable, never what it is: no Natick, no giveaway. Companion puzzle: The Keystone. Full write-up: docs/relational-difficulty.md.
